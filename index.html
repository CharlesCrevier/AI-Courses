1 — Executive Summary

Executive Summary — Participant Profile Overview

The June 2026 ITC-ILO portfolio brings together 82 professionals from 32 countries across five courses focused on labour governance, social protection, occupational safety, and digital transformation. The cohort reflects a deliberate effort to span institutional levels, geographic regions, and professional functions — providing a rich peer-learning environment anchored in real-world implementation experience.

Geographic reach is broad but strategically concentrated. Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for the largest share of participants (35%), with Nigeria (14), Ukraine (11), Cambodia (5), and Bangladesh (5) as the most represented countries. The portfolio extends across all major developing regions — Asia-Pacific, Latin America, Eastern Europe, MENA, and the Pacific — alongside staff from three international organisations (ILO, WFP, WHO).

Seniority is a defining feature of this cohort. The majority hold director, head, chief, or commissioner-level positions, indicating that institutions have invested in sending decision-makers rather than entry-level staff. This is most pronounced in the Labour Inspection and Case Management courses, where heads of government departments and national agencies predominate.

Institutional Profile
Government ministries and national social security agencies account for over 75% of participants. The remainder come from international organisations (10%), private sector and consulting firms (8%), and trade unions (4%) — a mix that brings both policy-making and operational perspectives to classroom discussions.
Digital & Technical Capacity
Approximately 18% of participants hold explicit IT, engineering, or software development roles — concentrated in the Social Protection/AI and OSH courses. The Case Management course draws the highest share of combined technical and senior profiles, suggesting strong institutional readiness to implement digital systems upon return.
Cross-Course Connectivity
Six countries — Nigeria, Malawi, Eswatini, Cameroon, Kenya, and Tanzania — send participants to more than one course simultaneously, creating natural institutional networks across thematic areas. This cross-course presence is an asset for peer exchange and potential inter-ministerial collaboration beyond the training period.
Key Opportunity
The seniority and institutional diversity of this cohort suggest high potential for knowledge transfer and policy influence upon return. Structured alumni follow-up and peer-network activation — particularly across the Africa and Asia-Pacific clusters — could significantly amplify the portfolio's long-term impact.
